From Phishing Email to Abuse Reports: Triaging a ScreenConnect MSI Campaign

2026-03-18 · ~7 min read
IR DFIR

A user forwarded a suspicious email to our team — subject line "Requesting Reviewing Document Via Adobe Cloud," apparently from a compliance manager at another tribal organization in Utah. Before we could look at it, a second user mentioned they'd already tried to download whatever it linked to and said "it wasn't compatible with windows." That phrase got my attention fast.

Names, domains, and identifiers have been sanitized.

Was the Sender Spoofed or Compromised?

This is always the first question. The email came from a real person at a real organization we've worked with before. If the account was spoofed, it's an external problem. If the account was compromised, someone is sitting in that person's mailbox right now sending phishing emails to their entire contact list.

The headers told the whole story:

  • Message-ID ended in @mail.yahoo.com — the email originated from real Yahoo infrastructure, not some random SMTP relay
  • X-Mailer: WebService/1.1.25380 YMailNorrin — Yahoo Mail web client. Someone logged into this account through a browser and hit send.
  • DKIM signature — signed by yahoo.com and valid. This is the key detail. If the email were spoofed, the DKIM signature would either fail validation or be signed by a completely different domain. A valid DKIM signature from Yahoo means Yahoo's servers authenticated the sender and sent the message.
  • MX records for the sender's domain pointed to mx-biz.mail.am0.yahoodns.net — Yahoo Small Business hosting. The domain's mail infrastructure is Yahoo, and the DKIM signature is Yahoo. Everything is consistent.
Verdict: compromised, not spoofed. The attacker has this person's credentials and sent this email from their actual Yahoo-hosted mailbox. DKIM passed because the email genuinely came from where it claims to come from.

Barracuda scored the email at -0.82 against a kill threshold of 3.0. DKIM passed. SPF passed. The only flag was a missing To: header, which added +1.21 — not nearly enough. The gateway was never going to catch this because, technically, it's a legitimate email from a legitimate mailbox. It just happens to be sent by an attacker.

The Payload

The phishing link in the email went through ctrk.klclick3[.]com — a Klaviyo email marketing tracking link. Klaviyo is a legitimate marketing platform; the attacker was abusing it for redirect infrastructure. The Klaviyo link redirected to dccyop[.]org/[redacted], which served up ScreenConnect.ClientSetup.msi — a 16.7 MB MSI installer.

The user who said it "wasn't compatible with windows" almost certainly hit a SmartScreen warning or a browser download block. That warning did its job.

Sandbox Analysis

I submitted the MSI to Tria.ge and got an 8/10 threat score. It's a legitimate ScreenConnect installer, but weaponized for unauthorized access:

  • C2 relay: instance-x7bxki-relay.screenconnect[.]com resolving to 15.235.110.89 (OVH Canada)
  • Session ID: c74a4df9-a1ae-422f-8ab0-a017da1ee909 hardcoded in the installer config
  • Credential provider DLL and authentication package DLL installed — these hook into the Windows credential pipeline for interception
  • 64 privilege escalation attempts — SeDebugPrivilege, SeTcbPrivilege, and more
  • 37+ COM hijacking registry modifications

This is the same RMM tool abuse pattern that's everywhere right now. The attacker doesn't need to write custom malware. They configure a ScreenConnect instance, bake the connection details into the MSI, and get persistent remote access through a signed, legitimate remote management tool. It installs as a service, survives reboots, and looks like normal IT software to anyone who isn't specifically looking for unauthorized RMM tools.

Endpoint Verification

Two machines received the email. I pulled AMP trajectory data for both via the API and ran quick checks for ScreenConnect artifacts: files, services, registry keys, network connections to the C2 relay. Both came back clean. One user downloaded the MSI but never ran it. The other never clicked the link at all.

No installation on either machine. No ScreenConnect files, no services, no registry entries, no connections to the C2 IP. SmartScreen and user caution did the job here.

The Abuse Reporting Chain

This is where I think a lot of IR stops short. We confirmed no compromise on our end, but the attacker's infrastructure is still live. Every hour that relay instance stays up, more orgs get hit with the same MSI. So I spent about 10 minutes filing abuse reports:

  • ConnectWise — reported the weaponized relay instance with the session ID. ConnectWise has an abuse process for this and can disable the instance remotely.
  • OVH — reported the C2 server IP (15.235.110.89) to their abuse team with the full context.
  • APWG (Anti-Phishing Working Group) — full IOC report to reportphishing@apwg.org. They aggregate phishing data across the industry.
  • Klaviyo — reported the abused tracking link to abuse@klaviyo.com. The redirect chain needs to be broken.
  • The compromised user's organization — contacted them directly to let them know the account is compromised and actively sending phishing emails.

And then there was Yahoo.

Yahoo's abuse reporting is broken. The sender's mailbox is compromised and actively being used to phish. I tried to report it. Yahoo's online abuse form is non-functional. I emailed abuse-mail@yahoo.com and got a 554 mailbox not found bounce. The largest email provider in the world and you literally cannot report a compromised account to them. The attacker will keep sending from this mailbox until the actual owner notices or Yahoo's automated systems eventually catch on — whichever comes first. I documented the failed reporting attempts and moved on.

Key Takeaways

  • DKIM tells you compromised vs. spoofed. If the DKIM signature is valid and the signing domain matches the sender's mail infrastructure, someone has the credentials. If DKIM fails or is signed by a random domain, it's spoofed. This distinction matters because it changes your response — compromised means the sender's org needs to be notified immediately.
  • Gateways can't catch compromised account phishing. Barracuda scored this at -0.82. The email was technically legitimate — it really did come from where it said it came from, DKIM passed, SPF passed. User awareness is the only defense layer for this attack pattern.
  • RMM tool abuse is the dominant delivery method right now. ScreenConnect, AnyDesk, Action1 — attackers use legitimate remote access tools because they're signed, trusted, and blend in with IT software. If you're not explicitly blocking unauthorized RMM tools, you're relying on users to not run the installer.
  • Report upstream. ConnectWise can disable the relay instance. OVH can null-route the C2 IP. Klaviyo can kill the redirect link. It takes 10 minutes and actually disrupts the attacker's infrastructure. Your incident is one of many — cutting off the tooling helps everyone.
  • Yahoo's abuse reporting is broken. Document it, move on.
